#  Data Visualization & Chart Selection

> **Learning Goal:** Learn how to select the right chart for the right data and translate raw numbers into a clear visual story.

---

##  Why Visualizations Matter

In Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA), our brains process visual information much faster than numbers in a table. 

- **Statistics can lie, but data shapes rarely do.** (See *Anscombe's Quartet*, where completely different data sets can have the exact same mean and correlation but look vastly different on a graph).
- **Stakeholders speak visually.** A CEO won't read your `.describe()` output, but they will instantly understand a bar chart showing declining sales.

> "A picture is worth a thousand words. A good data visualization is worth a thousand lines of Python code."

---

## ⚖️ Python's Visualization Tools

In the Data Analytics ecosystem, two libraries reign supreme:

### 1. Matplotlib (`plt`)
- The granddaddy of Python visualization
- Highly customizable — you can tweak every single pixel
- Can be wordy and complex to write for simple tasks

### 2. Seaborn (`sns`)
- Built *on top* of Matplotlib
- Extremely easy to write (one-liners for complex charts)
- Looks beautiful out-of-the-box
- Integrates flawlessly with Pandas DataFrames

**Our Strategy:** Use Seaborn for 90% of our daily plotting, and fall back to Matplotlib when we need to tweak formatting (like changing a title or saving the figure).

##  Chart Selection Guide

The hardest part of visualization isn't writing the code; it's choosing the right chart. Ask yourself: **What am I trying to show?**

### 1. Showing a Distribution (Single Variable)
When you have a single numerical column and want to see how the values are distributed.

- **Histogram (`sns.histplot`)**: Groups data into "bins" and counts the occurrences.
- **KDE Plot (`sns.kdeplot`)**: A smooth curve showing the density of the data.

> **Example:** "What is the distribution of student marks in the class?"

### 2. Comparing Categories
When you want to compare a numerical value across different categories.

- **Bar Chart (`sns.barplot` or `sns.countplot`)**: Best for comparing average values or counting occurrences per category.

> **Example:** "How many students passed vs. failed?" or "What is the average attendance for each city?"

### 3. Showing Relationships (Two Numerical Variables)
When you want to see if one number goes up, does the other go up too?

- **Scatter Plot (`sns.scatterplot`)**: Plots every single data point as a dot. Perfect for detecting clusters and correlation.
- **Line Chart (`sns.lineplot`)**: Best when there is a logical ordering to the x-axis, especially **Time** (time series data).

> **Example:** "Do students who study more get higher marks?" (Scatter) or "How have company sales changed over the last 12 months?" (Line)

### 4. Spotting Outliers & Spread
When you want to see the 5-number summary (Min, Q1, Median, Q3, Max) visually.

- **Boxplot (`sns.boxplot`)**: A standardized way of displaying the distribution based on quartiles.
- **Violin Plot (`sns.violinplot`)**: A combination of a boxplot and a KDE plot.

> **Example:** "Are there any students with abnormally low or high marks compared to the rest of the class?"

##  The Anatomy of a Good Plot

A plot without labels is useless. Every single time you make a chart, standard practice requires:
1. **A clear title**
2. **X-axis and Y-axis labels**
3. **A legend** (if distinguishing categories)
4. **Appropriate colours** (e.g., green for profit, red for loss)

```python
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

# Set a beautiful modern theme
sns.set_theme(style="whitegrid")

df = pd.read_csv("students_data.csv")

# 1. Create the base plot using Seaborn
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) # Set graph size (width, height)
sns.scatterplot(x="study_hours", y="marks", hue="city", data=df, s=100)

# 2. Format using Matplotlib
plt.title("Study Hours vs. Marks by City", fontsize=16, fontweight="bold")
plt.xlabel("Hours Studied per Week", fontsize=12)
plt.ylabel("Final Exam Marks", fontsize=12)
plt.legend(title="Location")

# 3. Show plot!
plt.show()
```

##  Real-World Example: Sales Team

> You are analyzing the Q1 sales performance of a retail store.
> - **Use a Line Chart** to show total sales volume from Jan to March.
> - **Use a Bar Chart** to compare the total sales generated by the North, South, and East regions.
> - **Use a Scatter Plot** to see if there is a relationship between marketing spend and product sales. 
> - **Use a Boxplot** to see if some sales reps are performing abnormally high or low relative to their peers.

---

## ✅ Key Takeaways

1. **Understand your question first.** Don't just make a chart because you can; make a chart to answer a specific question.
2. **Histograms/Boxplots** for understanding a single column.
3. **Scatterplots** for looking at the relationship between two numbers.
4. **Bar/Line Charts** for comparing categories or changes over time.
5. Always, always, always label your axes and give your chart a title!
